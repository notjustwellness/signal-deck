01  ·  How to say it

Pronounced sig-NAHL.

The brand reads like a dictionary entry because it is one. /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ is the International Phonetic Alphabet transcription of the word “signal” — the same notation a linguist or language teacher uses to show exact pronunciation.

Say it out loud: sig-NAHL. Stress on the second syllable. The “i” is short like in sit, the “a” is the long open vowel in father, and the final “l” is soft.

The spoken pronunciation guide

Why IPA as a wordmark

The IPA transcription is a brand move that says “we know what we are; we don’t need to translate ourselves.” It treats the brand name like a dictionary entry — sophisticated, confident, impossible to mispronounce, and unlike anything else in the CPG category. Where other brands rely on a stylized logotype, signal uses the very notation language teachers use to teach the word itself. The brackets make it unmistakable as notation, not text.

03  ·  Color

Three colors. Zero noise.

No gradients, no color-blocking, no secondary palettes. Total restraint. Warm across the entire spectrum — even the darkest color carries aubergine warmth, even the lightest has pink warmth. Nothing cold, nothing clinical.

signal
#AD4236  ·  rgb(173, 66, 54)
The hero color. Packet front, wordmark letters, CTAs, primary surface. Deep copper-red — warm, alive, confident. Between fire and dried blood.
rose linen
#D8CCC1  ·  rgb(246, 239, 234)
The warm neutral. Web backgrounds, interior packaging, wordmark IPA accent characters, body copy. Barely-there blush warmth — linen in morning light.
deep evening
#241B17  ·  rgb(74, 37, 64)
The dark anchor. Container color, dark slide backgrounds, the N°003 packet, wordmark on light, display fixtures. A deep warm aubergine — dusk, not midnight. The warmth in the dark is what keeps the palette alive, and the hue keeps it distinct from the hero red.

How the colors work together

Packet front: Signal surface + Deep Evening embossed type. Warm on warm.  Packet back: clear PET, product visible.  Container: Deep Evening exterior, Signal or Rose Linen accents.  Web / landing pages: Rose Linen backgrounds, Deep Evening text, Signal for CTAs and hero elements.  Social content: Signal as the signature color, Deep Evening for text overlays, Rose Linen for contrast and breathing room.

What to avoid

Never pure white (#FFFFFF) — too clinical; use Rose Linen instead.  Never pure black (#000000) — too cold; use Deep Evening instead.  Never pastels — soft-wellness coded.  Never earth tones or kraft — LMNT/granola territory.  Never neon accent colors — energy drink coded.  No gradients except the subtle radial glow that appears on cover-slide backgrounds.

All headlines lowercase — matches the lowercase “signal” voice. No serifs anywhere. No script or handwritten faces ever. 05  ·  Usage

Do. Don’t.

The wordmark is the most load-bearing element in the brand system. These rules exist to keep it consistent across every touchpoint, from slide decks to physical packaging to investor one-pagers.

Do

  • Use the canonical two-color treatment (Option A) as the default
  • Use Plus Jakarta Sans 700 as the wordmark font — always
  • Keep the wordmark lowercase, always, even at the start of sentences in visual contexts
  • Include aria-label=“signal” on every web wordmark so screen readers and copy-paste return the brand name
  • Respect clear space equal to the height of one lowercase “s” on all four sides
  • Use the IPA brackets — they are part of the wordmark, not decoration
  • Pair with the overline “send the right” on cover treatments

Don’t

  • Substitute Anguita Sans or any other font for the wordmark — Anguita Sans lacks the IPA glyphs
  • Capitalize the wordmark or any individual character
  • Rotate, skew, outline, drop-shadow, or distort the wordmark in any way
  • Recolor with anything outside the three-color palette
  • Place on busy photographic backgrounds — use a solid surface color
  • Use the written word “signal” (in Anguita Sans or otherwise) as the wordmark — the wordmark is always the IPA form
  • Remove the brackets or strip the IPA modifier characters (ˈ, ː)
  • Render at less than the minimum size (see next page)

The brand WORD vs. the visible WORDMARK

These are separate things. The brand word is still “signal” — used in spoken conversation, body copy, URLs, email addresses, legal documents, the master tagline “send the right signal,” and INSDR Wellness — the cellcare company references. The brand wordmark is the visible logo element: /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ in Plus Jakarta Sans, two-color. The IPA exception applies only to the visible logo, not to every occurrence of the word “signal” in copy.

06  ·  Clear Space & Minimum Size

Room to breathe.

The wordmark needs visual space around it equal to the height of one lowercase “s” character. No other element (text, image, border) may enter the clear space zone.

/sɪɡˈnɑːl/
Dashed rectangle represents the minimum clear-space zone. The small “s” marks at the corners show the unit of measure — the clear-space is the x-height of the wordmark’s lowercase “s.”

Minimum sizes

Digital: 120px wide minimum (enough to keep the IPA modifier characters readable at screen DPI).  Print (business cards, letterhead): 1.2 inches wide minimum.  Packaging (packet front): 0.9 inches wide minimum.  Favicon / app icon: do NOT use the full IPA wordmark — use a single-character “s” or the upcoming monogram instead. The IPA wordmark is not legible at 32×32px or below.

07  ·  Physical Applications

The wordmark on physical objects.

The IPA wordmark is engineered to scale beyond screens. Debossed, etched, or printed onto industrial surfaces, it carries the same load-bearing role — quiet, decode-rewarding, and never decorative. The Travel Container is the first non-packet hardware surface to carry the wordmark; the same rules apply to any future physical application.

The Travel Container — example application

Matte red anodized aluminum body. Woven black mesh grille. IPA wordmark /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ debossed in soft type on the lower red panel, signal-light against the matte surface. A tactile push button opens the compact. Universal MagSafe; phone-attached.

Notice what stays consistent with the screen rules: lowercase, IPA brackets retained, modifier characters (ˈ ː) preserved, two-color treatment carried over via the debossing depth and the matte/light surface contrast. Notice what adapts: no screen-DPI minimum applies; the wordmark sits at roughly 0.9 inches wide, matching the packet-front minimum.

Rules for physical applications

  • Production technique drives the “color” treatment. Debossing, laser etching, foil stamping, screen printing, and metal anodizing all stand in for the two-color screen rule. The IPA brackets and modifier characters must remain visually distinct from the body characters.
  • Surface color governs. On signal-red surfaces, the wordmark debosses to read in signal-light tone (as on the Travel Container). On Rose Linen or Deep Evening surfaces, the same logic applies.
  • Clear-space rule still applies. Maintain the height of one lowercase “s” on all four sides — on hardware, this means no other tactile feature (button, vent, magnet ring outline) may enter the zone.
  • Minimum physical size: ~0.9 inches wide. Below this, the IPA modifier characters lose legibility under most production techniques.
  • Never re-letter for the medium. Don't switch the wordmark to a more “industrial” font for hardware. The IPA wordmark IS the wordmark; production technique adapts to it, not the other way around.

08  ·  Trademark Brief  ·  Part One

What you’re up against.

Research, not legal advice. The analysis on pages 9–10 is a data brief prepared for strategic planning. It is not a trademark clearance, it is not legal advice, and it does not substitute for a formal knockout search or attorney consultation. Consult a qualified US trademark attorney before filing any application.

The core problem

“Signal” is a common 6-letter English noun with decades of commercial use across many USPTO classes. That makes it arbitrary when applied to a dietary supplement chew (good — arbitrary marks are theoretically registrable), but also crowded (bad — registering a common word in a saturated class is one of the hardest things to do at the USPTO).

The Big Three conflicts to know about

1. Signal Messenger (Signal Technology Foundation) — Reg. 5230850

Registered in Classes 9, 38, 42 for encrypted messaging software. A Class 5 dietary supplement is far enough removed that a 2(d) likelihood-of-confusion refusal is unlikely under the standard DuPont analysis. The real risk is softer: Signal Messenger arguably qualifies as a famous mark among tech-literate consumers, which expands protection beyond registered classes under dilution theory. Signal Foundation has sent cease-and-desist letters before. Watchable, not blocking.

2. Signal toothpaste (Unilever)

Massive global oral-care brand dating to 1961, sold in Europe, the Middle East, and Asia. The key fact: Unilever divested its US oral care portfolio in 2003 (Church & Dwight bought Mentadent, Pepsodent, Aim; Signal was never a meaningful US brand). There is no active Signal toothpaste in US commerce today. That likely means Unilever has no enforceable US Class 3 rights to block a US wellness brand — but an attorney must confirm that no zombie US registration or recent intent-to-use filing exists on TSDR. If the US registration is dead, this is a non-issue. If it’s alive, it’s a serious Class 3 problem.

3. The unknown Class 5 or Class 29/30 supplement brand

Statistically, the real risk is not the famous marks — it’s some small registered supplement, functional food, or candy brand named “SIGNAL [something]” that didn’t surface in web-search summaries but exists on USPTO TESS. Roughly 1 in 5 USPTO applications receive a 2(d) likelihood-of-confusion refusal (source: Gerben IP), and that rate climbs sharply for short common English words in crowded consumer classes. A paid attorney knockout search — $500–800 — is the highest-leverage spend before any filing.

Likelihood assessment

StrategyLikelihoodWhy
Bare SIGNAL word mark in Class 5 LOW to VERY LOW Common word in a crowded class. Existing PST Vet compound mark in Class 005. Near-certain office action on likelihood-of-confusion or descriptiveness grounds. Possible, but slow and expensive.
Stylized IPA design mark /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ MODERATE Design marks are narrower in scope but easier to register. The IPA bracketing is genuinely unusual — no CPG competitor uses phonetic transcription as its primary wordmark. That unusualness is itself a distinctiveness argument.
INSDR SIGNAL or SIGNAL BY INSDR word mark MODERATE to MODERATE–HIGH A house-mark compound substantially reduces 2(d) risk because the first word (INSDR) is a unique coinage and examiners weigh the mark as a whole. This is the recommended starting path.

Recommended mitigation strategy

File two applications in parallel: (1) “INSDR SIGNAL” as a standard-character word mark in Class 5, and (2) the stylized IPA /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ wordmark as a design mark in Class 5, with defensive filings in Class 3 (cosmetics) and Class 30 (confectionery) if budget permits. The word-mark filing ducks most likelihood-of-confusion refusals because INSDR is a novel coinage; the design mark locks down the visual identity. Combined cost: ~$700 government + $1,800 attorney = $2,500 for the core filings, or ~$4,500 for multi-class defensive coverage. Combined likelihood: MODERATE to MODERATE–HIGH.

The honest bottom line

Bare “SIGNAL” as a standard-character word mark in Class 5 is a hard filing. You’re fighting a crowded class, a common English word, a famous tech mark in adjacent classes, and a global Unilever toothpaste that may or may not still have US paper on file. Do not file the bare word mark first. Do two things, in this order: (1) Pay $500–800 for an attorney knockout search on SIGNAL + SIGNL + phonetic variants in Classes 3/5/29/30/32 — this is the single highest-leverage spend before filing, and resolves the real question in 48 hours. (2) If the knockout is clean, file “INSDR SIGNAL” + the IPA /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ design mark, both in Class 5 ITU, with defensive filings in Class 3 and 30. Budget ~$4,500 all-in, timeline ~16 months to registration. Do not publicly launch or demo the final brand until the ITU is filed and the priority date is locked. Do not file before talking to a US trademark attorney.
