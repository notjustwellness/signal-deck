The honest headline

Signal is a premium functional-chew brand selling one product at $89/month. Chews built on branded actives cost more to make than capsules — an early benchmark feared ~35–45% gross margin. A bottom-up estimate, anchored by three real ingredient quotes (Kaneka QH® ubiquinol, MitoPrime® ergothioneine at $980/kg, creatine at $11/kg), lands well above that.

~73% gross margin. ~58% contribution margin. ~$52 left to cover the cost of winning a customer — on every box.

Why the benchmark was wrong: the premium actives are milligram-dosed, so they cost little; the one gram-scale active (creatine) is a cheap commodity. The figures below are an estimate to be confirmed by the validated formulation quote — locking that quote is the explicit first use of seed funds.

Two metrics, kept distinct (don't conflate them): Gross margin = revenue − COGS only (~73% here). Contribution margin = revenue − all per-unit variable cost, including fulfillment, parcel, and payment processing (~58% here). The ~58% is the one that has to cover the cost of acquiring a customer (CAC). Folding creatine into the single product nudged COGS up ~$1.65/box vs the prior hero-only figure (~$22 → ~$24), trimming gross from ~75% to ~73% — marginal, because creatine is a cheap commodity.

2How we protect & improve the margin

  1. Cost came in low. The milligram-dosed premium actives — and switching spermidine to NNB's Puremidine™ (~3 mg vs an 800 mg wheat-germ extract) — are why. Validation turns the estimate into a fact.
  2. Premium price headroom. $89 is the floor; the positioning supports $99–120 (AG1 sits at ~$99). Any price move is upside on top of these margins.
  3. Manufacturing scale. Moving order volume from 10K → 25–50K units pulls per-unit cost down ~20–40% (Zone Halo's own read).
  4. Lean packaging. Kraft outer + a signal-red paperboard petal-fold tray each month — low cost, no durable hardware to amortize.

3Repeat economics — the model that matters

Signal is a subscription: monthly auto-replenish, cancel anytime in one tap — the structure that matches a daily-practice product. The whole business turns on re-order, so the number to watch is retained cycles, not first purchases.
