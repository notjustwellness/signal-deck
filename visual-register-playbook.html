§I — The Brief

One word.
Still.

Every subject we cast, every room we shoot in, every artifact we frame answers a single sentence: still ____. Still here. Still himself. Still ready. Still doing the thing. The word collapses the brand thesis into a casting prompt. If a subject can't finish that sentence, they're not ours.

What "still" rejects

"Still" is not vital, not youthful, not optimized, not energized. Those are achievement registers. They announce. "Still" doesn't announce. It registers a person who didn't quit, didn't pivot, didn't get replaced by their own optimization. The body kept showing up. The foundation held.

You don't feel a foundation. You feel everything that stands on it.
§II — The Antithesis

One antithesis.
That's the discipline.

Most brands trying to escape wellness aesthetics try to invent a new opposite. The category already has too many opposites. The brand needs one — clean, specific, repeatable. Naming it once means we never have to perform "anti-wellness" later. We just shoot.

The antithesis

The antithesis is wellness-and-influencer imagery. Bodies presented as objects of evaluation. Performance staged as outcome. Aspiration presented as identity. The morning-routine feed. The before-and-after. The smile that is the product.

Signal refuses this register because the customer is the subject of the practice, not the object of a photo. The body is not the proof. The work is. What we document is continuation, not aspiration.

Signal's anti-wellness isn't anti-effort. It's anti-performance of effort.

What this rules out that "anti-wellness" alone doesn't

"Anti-wellness" gets you to ban the green-juice influencer. It does not get you to ban the goblin-mode parody who's performing for the same audience in reverse. Both are still inside the wellness conversation. Signal isn't in the conversation at all.

We don't argue with the feed. We make something the feed can't render.
§III — The Canonical Format

The format is the canon.
Not a film.

This is the reference. Not a single film. Not a campaign. The brand's primary format itself is the canon — what every shoot is judged against. Camera = the subject's eyes. Approximately 30 seconds of a daily situation. Signal lands as a single beat inside it. Raw. Unstaged. Ambient.

No single sequence is canonical, and on purpose. The variety is the discipline. What's canonical is the format — first-person, half a minute, the body out of frame, the routine continuing.

What happens in 30 seconds

The subject is doing something they already do. Coffee gets made. The dog gets walked. The kid is on the floor. The packet is picked up in the middle of it — not at the start, not at the end. The chew snaps. The hand sets the empty packet down. The routine continues.

There is no climax. There is no slow-motion reveal. The product appears the way real objects appear inside real days: briefly, in passing, without ceremony. The beat lasts maybe four seconds out of thirty.

Signal is a punctuation mark inside the routine. Not the sentence.

What rotates between films

Different morning. Different counter. Different time of year. Sometimes the subject is alone; sometimes a partner is in the kitchen; sometimes a kid is awake; sometimes a friend is on the phone. The world is populated.

When others appear, dialogue is allowed. It can be unhurried, even playful — the warmth of a real moment, not the airless register of brand voice. The discipline: the dialogue resolves back to the world Signal lives in. Foundation. Real food. Real doses. The practice. The line never breaks into brand-cute or lifestyle-cute; it sits inside the substrate register we already keep.

The film closes with the routine still happening. The chew is taken; the day keeps moving.

Dialogue is part of the world. It never becomes the world.

When other formats are allowed

Stills are allowed in support of the primary format. Where possible, they are framed first-person — the hand reaching, the packet on the counter, the coffee beside it. Stills are not the format; they are extracts from it.

Third-person framing — the rare brand-identity surface, the editorial feature, the founder note — is allowed when the surface needs it. It is never the primary register. Product shots and packaging photography live in their own register inside §VII; they are not casting calls and they are not script.

Other formats serve the primary. They never replace it.

Production briefs carry the storyboards and shot scripts. The playbook holds the format.

§IV — The Gesture

The hands do the gesture.
No one performs.

In first-person format, the body is out of frame. What's left in frame is the hands. They're the only part of the subject the viewer sees. The gesture lives in what the hands do as the situation unfolds, with Signal as a single beat inside it.

The lock

What stays constant across every film: hands are doing the routine, not posing for it. No reaching for the camera. No styled placement. No slow-motion product reveals. The hand picks up the packet the way the hand picks up a coffee cup — at the speed real life moves.

What rotates: which routine, which counter, which time of day. The hand always belongs to the subject's situation. We don't import hands from a hand-model.

The hands keep doing what they were doing. Signal enters and leaves.

What the hands are doing

Picking up the packet. Snapping the dual-chew between thumb and finger. Setting the tray on the counter. Twisting the foil. Setting the empty packet down. Carrying on. The chew goes in mid-sentence, mid-task, mid-walk. Never as a standalone beat. Never the climax of the film.

Signal is a punctuation mark inside the routine. Not the sentence.
§V — Casting

We cast situations.
The body just shows up.

The cast is whoever the situation is. In first-person format, the viewer never sees the subject's face — the casting question stops being "what does the body look like" and becomes "whose 30 seconds is this." We pick the situation first. The person is whoever lives inside it.

What we still won't cast

Wellness influencers. Optimized bodies built for the camera. Styled faces. Anyone whose hands wouldn't make sense in the routine we're filming. The face isn't visible, but the hands are — and hands that have never done the work look wrong even out of focus.

The customer is the subject of the practice, not the object of a photo.

The casting test

One short sentence answers what this 30 seconds is: still ____. Still working. Still up. Still showing up for the kid. Still on schedule. The line is cast-agnostic — it describes the moment, not the person. If the situation can't be named in one short still-line, we don't film it.

If the situation can be named in one sentence, it can be filmed.

Production briefs carry the enumerated situations and casting calls. The playbook holds the filter.

§VI — Environment

The room we see.
From where they stand.

The camera lives where the subject's eyes live. The room is never a backdrop being framed — it's the world the subject is inside, at the heights and angles their body actually meets. Countertop is countertop-height. The sink is at chest. The doorframe is overhead. We see what they see, in the order they see it.

The four conditions

Warm interior light. Tungsten, sun-through-window, table lamps. Never daylight-balanced cold. The world should feel like late afternoon, indoors, in a place that's been lived in.

Walls that hold history. Framed photographs, working tools, books that have been read, memorabilia in a stack. The wall is the second character. The viewer's eye passes them the way the subject's eye does — in glances, not portraits.

Real rooms, not sets. No styled backdrops. No rented locations dressed for editorial. The room belongs to the situation the film is documenting. If we can't find it, we find a different situation.

Continuity between the room and what the hands reach for. The packet sits where it would actually sit — next to the coffee, by the sink, on the side table. Not center-frame. Not styled into a flat lay. The hand reaches for it because that's where it lives.

The room is consistent with whose 30 seconds this is.

Environment archetypes

Memorabilia wall. Frames stacked. Decades layered.

Working room. Kitchen used daily. Workshop with tools where they live. Studio with materials mid-use.

Edge of weather. Porch, dock, doorway. Light spilling in.

Inherited room. Furniture older than the subject. The grandmother's chair.

What the lens has to honor

The hands are at the height the hands are. The film never tilts up to make the room look bigger or down to make it look more cinematic. The format is faithful to how the subject inhabits the room — not how a director would shoot it. If the kitchen is small, the kitchen is small.

If the room could only exist because we built it, we built the wrong thing.
§VII — Artifact Grammar

The decoded talisman.

The single most important section of this playbook. An artifact is anything in the brand's world that carries a sign without announcing it — a small object that's legible to insiders, invisible to everyone else. The IPA wordmark is one. The packet front is one. The tray is one. The annual coin — this section's anchor — is one. The brand's world is built from artifacts, not logos.

The principle

A logo asks to be recognized. An artifact asks to be decoded. The first is announcement; the second is invitation. Signal does not announce. Every object the brand puts into the world — and every object it accepts from a subject's life — must pass the same test: does this read as belonging to a system, without spelling the system out?

Authority is what others claim for you. The brand's job is to give them the artifact to claim it with.

What makes an artifact Signal

It carries a sign in our vocabulary — IPA phonetic notation, traditional Chinese script, the SKU palette (Rose Linen / Signal Red / Deep Evening / cream), a specific geometry, a specific material (paperboard, frosted acetate, brushed metal, cellulose acetate).

It looks used, not new. Patinated. Worn. The artifact is at its best the second decade you've owned it.

It reads as personal, not promotional. It does not look like merch. It looks like something you'd keep in a drawer, carry in a pocket, hand to a child.

It can be inherited. The artifact survives a lifetime. The packet does not. The coin does.

Three tiers of artifact

Tier 1 — Brand-canonical
The permanent objects.

The IPA wordmark /sɪɡˈnɑːl/. The packet front (color per SKU, no English). The paperboard tray + four frosted acetate dividers (D1: /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ variant cut, D4: /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ horizontal). The SaS DECODE puzzle card. These exist whether or not a customer engages. They are the brand's standing language.

Tier 2 — Owned by the customer
The objects that travel.

Objects the customer acquires through use — never additive to the product. The Travel Container (the daily carry). The SaS card (the gifting mechanism). The wax seal closing each letter. And at the end of Year I, the annual coin. These move from brand hands to customer hands and become theirs. Merch lives in a separate bucket — selection-criteria gated, not banned. Restraint defines what enters either bucket.

Tier 3 — Found in the image
The reverse-causality move.

The most important tier — and the one that changes how we cast. We do not plant Signal artifacts on subjects. We cast people who already wear things — chains, watches, rings, medals, saint cards. Different for each one. The green object on the canonical's chain wasn't planted; he was wearing it when we found him. That's the proof: the brand's grammar is already in the world. Our job is to find it, frame it, and let the viewer decode it.

Inheritability — the §IX bridge

The manifesto's §IX hero is "Foundation in one life. Passed to the next." Artifacts are how that line becomes a physical fact. A packet is consumed; it cannot be passed down. A coin can. A stack of coins definitely can. The wordmark, etched on something durable, can. The artifact catalogue should be evaluated, in part, by what can be inherited. If an object cannot survive the subject, it doesn't carry the §IX promise.

Foundation in one life. Passed to the next. The artifact is the proof on the chain.

The annual coin — the anchor

The first physical artifact Signal mints. Not a product. Not a giveaway. Not a welcome gift. Earned by completing one full year of practice. Hand-cast metal — brushed brass, hand-cast bronze, or matte silver/nickel (render v0.1 below, in silver) — ~38mm diameter, ~3mm thick. The IPA wordmark /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ deeply etched on the obverse, framed by a thin debossed inner rim. A Roman numeral year (I, II, III, V, X, XXV) on the reverse. Identical design across years; only the year-mark changes — like commemorative annual releases. Plain or lightly-reeded edge. Patinates with the hand that holds it.

02 — Year I / render v0.1

The first earned artifact.

Year I obverse. The notation IS the mark — eight-tenths of the face. No logo, no English brand name, no decorative flourish. The reverse carries only the Roman numeral.

Patinates with handling — what the customer earns by Year V looks measurably different from what they earned by Year I. The collection becomes the proof. Material in flight: brass / bronze / matte silver, all live. Render shown in silver.

Year-one-or-nothing

There is no day-30 token. No 90-day welcome chip. No half-year tease. If you don't make it to one year, you get nothing physical. That's the discipline. The coin's gravity comes from being hard to reach. Pre-Year-I rewards would dilute it. The threshold is the point.

You don't get rewarded for going halfway.

The annual ritual

On the customer's anniversary, one small kraft paperboard box arrives. Inside: the year's coin. The box closes with a deep Signal-red wax stamp of the IPA glyph cluster pressed onto the lid. Once a year, every year the customer stays. The seal closes; the coin marks. Nothing else needs to be in the box.

After five years, the customer has a small stack — five coins, five boxes, the years that came with them. Tenure made physical. Time you can hold.

Foundation in one life. Passed to the next. The coin is the chain.

Supporting artifacts

The wax seal. SaS letters and the annual box close with a deep Signal-red wax seal stamped with the IPA glyph cluster /sɪɡˈnɑːl/. Tactile. Decoded. Worth keeping for the seal alone.

The folded photograph — conditional. Considered for the Year-I envelope: an archival print of the canonical visual-register image (§III), small, framable. Kept only if it functions as the visual register made physical — not as gift-box delight.

The selection-criteria filter

The brand commissions objects across four buckets: operational (the packet, the tray, the dividers, the Travel Container), practice-carrier (the SaS card), recognition (the annual coin), and merch (allowed under three criteria). A piece enters the merch bucket only if it clears all three: (a) deeper meaning than decoration — the piece is about something specific, not the brand's name pasted on a category-default object; (b) something specific — it names a particular thing the brand owns; (c) something the rest of the brand cannot say — it exists because the manifesto, the packet, the playbook, the codex can't carry what it carries.

Pieces that fail any criterion don't enter the catalogue. Whatever the canonical subject — or any subject — wears stays a casting cue (Tier 3, §V): we cast people who already wear something personal, different for each one. The casting register doesn't depend on what the brand sells.

The world is built from objects that say something the rest of the brand cannot say otherwise.

What fails the criteria

Logo on a thing — t-shirts, stickers, hoodies, mugs, totes, keychains, magnets — fails all three criteria. The brand's name pasted on a category-default object announces; it doesn't carry. Anything that reads as promotional rather than personal. Anything that looks new in a way that's bragging.

Logo on a thing is not a thing.

The artifact appearance rule

An artifact appears in an image when it would already be there. Never as a prop. Never as a foreground subject. If the subject's hand happens to be near the tray, that's a frame. If we move the tray into the hand, that's a product shot — and we don't take product shots in the editorial register. The artifact is decoded by being incidental.

The brand never says "look at this." The viewer says, "I see it."
§VIII — Decoding Posture

No text on the image.
Ever.

A Signal image carries a person, a room, and at most one decoded artifact. It does not carry a headline, a caption, a tagline, a CTA, a product name, a price, a claim, or a brand mark. Captioning, if it happens at all, happens elsewhere — the line below, the next slide, the wall placard. The image's job is to be looked at, not read.

What this protects

Two things. First, the brand's world stays a world — not a marketing surface. Second, the viewer's first encounter with the image is theirs to make sense of. We don't tell them what to feel. They figure it out. That moment of figuring-it-out is what creates ownership.

Consistency is the new persuasion. We don't argue. We recur.
§IX — Banned

What this is not.

A negative-space list. If a frame leans in any of these directions, stop shooting and reset. None of these are subjective calls — they are the visual category default Signal exists to refuse.
  • The influencer pose. Hand on neck. Arched back. Lips parted. Half-smile to camera. Anything that reads "I am being photographed."
  • Studio white-wall portrait. Seamless paper. Daylight-balanced softbox. Catalog framing.
  • Gym selfie / gym mirror. Anything that frames the body as an optimization project.
  • AARP-coded "vital senior." Older subjects shown as inspirational specimens of aging well. Patronizing. Not what we do.
  • Before-and-after composition. Any structural promise of transformation.
  • Bottle in hand / product-in-hand. The subject holding the packet, the tray, the pendant up to the camera. That's a product shot. Different register.
  • Brand t-shirt or logo merch visible. No Signal-branded apparel in frame. Ever. (See §VII banned.)
  • Smiling-at-camera with perfect teeth. Performative warmth. Not ours.
  • Lifestyle props that don't decode. Generic mug, generic candle, generic plant. If a prop doesn't carry a sign, it's clutter.
  • Captions burned into the image. No headline overlay. No watermark. No "Signal." in the corner.
