§I — The Brief

One word.
Still.

Every subject we cast, every room we shoot in, every artifact we frame answers a single sentence: still ____. Still here. Still himself. Still ready. Still doing the thing. The word collapses the brand thesis into a casting prompt. If a subject can't finish that sentence, they're not ours.

What "still" rejects

"Still" is not vital, not youthful, not optimized, not energized. Those are achievement registers. They announce. "Still" doesn't announce. It registers a person who didn't quit, didn't pivot, didn't get replaced by their own optimization. The body kept showing up. The foundation held.

You don't feel a foundation. You feel everything that stands on it.

What "still" unlocks

A 75-year-old is not the only answer — but a 75-year-old is the answer that makes the thesis impossible to misread. The longer the life on the body, the louder the proof. Younger subjects can pass the test, but they have to earn it on a different axis (skill, weather, hands, time-spent-doing).

§II — The Canonical Image

One image.
All of it.

This is the reference. Not a campaign. Not a one-off. The register. Every casting decision, every location scout, every prop on a counter is judged against this frame. If we shoot something that doesn't share a parent with this image, it doesn't belong in the visual world.

The thesis made visible.

Why this works

The image gives you a person and a room. It does not give you a product, a claim, a caption, a CTA, or a body-position. The viewer arrives with no instructions and leaves with one read: he's still cool. Nothing was announced. Everything was decoded.

Build a world. Invite them in. Let them decode it.
§III — The Gesture

Gesture carries the attitude.
The prop is interchangeable.

The cigar is not load-bearing. What's load-bearing is the gesture: something held casually in the mouth, while the body remains unconcerned with the camera. James Dean held toothpicks. Cowboys held hay stalks. Sailors held matchsticks. The cigar is one option, not the option.

The lock

What stays constant: mouth occupied, body relaxed, eyes obscured, no attempt to charm. That's the cool-guy archetype. It predates tobacco by a century.

What rotates: the object itself. Use whichever fits the subject and the surface.

Sanctioned props

Cigar. Maximum attitude. Commits the brand to a "we don't moralize" position. Defensible — but choose it on purpose, not by accident. Comes with platform-ads and retailer-policy ripple. Reserve for editorial and brand-identity surfaces, not product pages.

Toothpick. Safe default. Same gesture, zero category risk. James Dean / mid-century-Americana register. Works on any surface.

Matchstick / unlit cigar stub. Reads as worn-in, tactile, slightly transgressive without committing the brand.

Blade of grass, stem of wheat, pencil. Situational. Rural, work-coded, or studio-coded. Use when the subject's world supports it.

Nothing at all. Closed mouth, jaw set, sunglasses doing the work. Always available. Never wrong.

The attitude is in the body, not the object. The object is the carrier.
§IV — Casting

Bodies that earned their state.

We don't cast wellness influencers. We don't cast optimized bodies. We cast people whose bodies carry their lives — hands that did things, posture that holds a story, skin that's been outside, frames that can't be cleanly slotted into a wellness demographic. The body should look used in the right way, not engineered.

The casting test

Every subject answers still ____ in one short sentence. If they can't, we don't shoot them. Examples below — the still-line is the brief; the rest is supporting detail.

Moments, not endpoints

The cast is not a progression toward one ideal age, one ideal body, one final state. It's a constellation of moments across one life. The 75-year-old isn't where you're going; he's what one moment of one life looks like. So is the 28-year-old line cook. So is the 60-year-old in the workshop. Each archetype below is a moment in time — none is the answer; all of them hold the same foundation underneath.

The viewer doesn't have to "see themselves in" any single subject. They have to feel that Signal sees them in many phases of their own life. The cast isn't a destination. It's a mirror at different angles.

Each character brings their own

Every subject we cast wears something personal — a chain, a watch, a ring, a bracelet, a medal, an old saint card on a string, a single earring. Different for each character. Their own, not ours. We never plant Signal-branded objects on the cast. We never standardize what they wear. We find people whose existing objects already carry meaning, and we let those objects appear in frame because they're already there.

The brand's grammar is already in the world. Our job is to find it.

§V — Environment

The room holds a life.

We don't shoot in studios. We don't shoot in white spaces. We shoot in rooms that already belong to the subject — rooms that hold their decades, their work, their family, their habits. The subject and the room are the same age. The room is not lit for us. We accept how it is.

The four conditions

Warm interior light. Tungsten, sun-through-window, table lamps. Never daylight-balanced cold. The world should feel like late afternoon, indoors, in a place that's been lived in.

Walls that hold history. Framed photographs, working tools, books that have been read, memorabilia in a stack. The wall is the second character.

Real rooms, not sets. No styled backdrops, no rented locations dressed for editorial. The room must belong to the subject, or someone like them. If we can't find it, we go find a different subject.

The subject and the room belong to each other. If you removed the subject, the room would still be coherent. If you removed the room, the subject would still be coherent. Neither is a prop for the other.

Environment archetypes

Memorabilia wall. Racetrack, sailing club, dive bar, family hallway, garage workshop. Frames stacked. Decades layered.

Working room. Kitchen used daily, workshop with tools where they live, studio with materials in mid-use. Mess is welcome.

Edge of weather. Porch, dock, doorway. The interior meets the outside. Light spills in. The subject is between.

Inherited room. A room that's been in the family. Furniture older than the subject. The grandmother's chair.

If the room could only exist because we built it, we built the wrong thing.
§VI — Artifact Grammar

The decoded talisman.

The single most important section of this playbook. An artifact is anything in the brand's world that carries a sign without announcing it — a small object that's legible to insiders, invisible to everyone else. The IPA wordmark is one. The packet front is one. The tray is one. The annual coin — this section's anchor — is one. The brand's world is built from artifacts, not logos.

The principle

A logo asks to be recognized. An artifact asks to be decoded. The first is announcement; the second is invitation. Signal does not announce. Every object the brand puts into the world — and every object it accepts from a subject's life — must pass the same test: does this read as belonging to a system, without spelling the system out?

Authority is what others claim for you. The brand's job is to give them the artifact to claim it with.

What makes an artifact Signal

It carries a sign in our vocabulary — IPA phonetic notation, traditional Chinese script, the SKU palette (Rose Linen / Signal Red / Deep Evening / cream), a specific geometry, a specific material (paperboard, frosted acetate, brushed metal, cellulose acetate).

It looks used, not new. Patinated. Worn. The artifact is at its best the second decade you've owned it.

It reads as personal, not promotional. It does not look like merch. It looks like something you'd keep in a drawer, carry in a pocket, hand to a child.

It can be inherited. The artifact survives a lifetime. The packet does not. The coin does.

Three tiers of artifact

Tier 1 — Brand-canonical
The permanent objects.

The IPA wordmark /sɪɡˈnɑːl/. The packet front (color per SKU, no English). The paperboard tray + four frosted acetate dividers (D1: 治未病 vertical, D4: /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ horizontal). The SaS DECODE puzzle card. These exist whether or not a customer engages. They are the brand's standing language.

Tier 2 — Owned by the customer
The objects that travel.

Objects the customer acquires through use — never sold separately, never additive. The Travel Container (the daily carry). The SaS card (the gifting mechanism). The wax seal closing each letter. And at the end of Year I, the annual coin. These move from brand hands to customer hands and become theirs. Anything beyond this list drifts toward merch. Restraint is the discipline.

Tier 3 — Found in the image
The reverse-causality move.

The most important tier — and the one that changes how we cast. We do not plant Signal artifacts on subjects. We cast people who already wear things — chains, watches, rings, medals, saint cards. Different for each one. The green object on the canonical's chain wasn't planted; he was wearing it when we found him. That's the proof: the brand's grammar is already in the world. Our job is to find it, frame it, and let the viewer decode it.

Inheritability — the §IX bridge

The manifesto's §IX hero is "Foundation in one life. Passed to the next." Artifacts are how that line becomes a physical fact. A packet is consumed; it cannot be passed down. A coin can. A stack of coins definitely can. The wordmark, etched on something durable, can. The artifact catalogue should be evaluated, in part, by what can be inherited. If an object cannot survive the subject, it doesn't carry the §IX promise.

Foundation in one life. Passed to the next. The artifact is the proof on the chain.

The annual coin — the anchor

The first physical artifact Signal mints. Not a product. Not a giveaway. Not a welcome gift. Earned by completing one full year of practice. Hand-cast metal — brushed brass, hand-cast bronze, or matte silver/nickel (render v0.1 below, in silver) — ~38mm diameter, ~3mm thick. The IPA wordmark /sɪɡˈnɑːl/ deeply etched on the obverse, framed by a thin debossed inner rim. A Roman numeral year (I, II, III, V, X, XXV) on the reverse. Identical design across years; only the year-mark changes — like commemorative annual releases. Plain or lightly-reeded edge. Patinates with the hand that holds it.

02 — Year I / render v0.1

The first earned artifact.

Year I obverse. The notation IS the mark — eight-tenths of the face. No logo, no English brand name, no decorative flourish. The reverse carries only the Roman numeral.

Patinates with handling — what the customer earns by Year V looks measurably different from what they earned by Year I. The collection becomes the proof. Material in flight: brass / bronze / matte silver, all live. Render shown in silver.

Year-one-or-nothing

There is no day-30 token. No 90-day welcome chip. No half-year tease. If you don't make it to one year, you get nothing physical. That's the discipline. The coin's gravity comes from being hard to reach. Pre-Year-I rewards would dilute it. The threshold is the point.

You don't get rewarded for going halfway.

The annual ritual

On the customer's anniversary, one envelope arrives. Inside: the year's coin. Sealed with a deep Signal-red wax stamp of the IPA glyph cluster. Once a year, every year the customer stays. The seal closes; the coin marks. Nothing else needs to be in the envelope.

After five years, the customer has a small stack — five coins, the years that came with them, the seals if they kept them. Tenure made physical. Time you can hold.

Foundation in one life. Passed to the next. The coin is the chain.

Supporting artifacts

The wax seal. SaS letters and the annual envelope close with a deep Signal-red wax seal stamped with the IPA glyph cluster /sɪɡˈnɑːl/. Tactile. Decoded. Worth keeping for the seal alone.

The folded photograph — conditional. Considered for the Year-I envelope: an archival print of the §IX canonical visual-register image, small, framable. Kept only if it functions as the visual register made physical — not as gift-box delight.

What we don't make

No pendant. No ceramic vessel. No candle. No glass. No tote. No keychain. No engraved key fob. No anything additive to what's operationally necessary. The artifacts of Signal are the objects the brand must produce to operate the product and recognize tenure. Anything beyond that drifts toward lifestyle merch and dilutes the world.

Whatever the canonical subject — or any subject — wears stays a casting cue (Tier 3, §IV): we cast people who already wear something personal, different for each one. We never sell them what to wear and never standardize it.

The world is built from objects the brand must produce, not the objects it could produce.

Banned as artifact

Logo merch. Brand t-shirts. Stickers. Anything that spells Signal or INSDR in Latin letters. Anything that announces. Anything that reads as promotional rather than personal. Anything that looks new in a way that's bragging.

The artifact appearance rule

An artifact appears in an image when it would already be there. Never as a prop. Never as a foreground subject. If the subject's hand happens to be near the tray, that's a frame. If we move the tray into the hand, that's a product shot — and we don't take product shots in the editorial register. The artifact is decoded by being incidental.

The brand never says "look at this." The viewer says, "I see it."
§VII — Decoding Posture

No text on the image.
Ever.

A Signal image carries a person, a room, and at most one decoded artifact. It does not carry a headline, a caption, a tagline, a CTA, a product name, a price, a claim, or a brand mark. Captioning, if it happens at all, happens elsewhere — the line below, the next slide, the wall placard. The image's job is to be looked at, not read.

What this protects

Two things. First, the brand's world stays a world — not a marketing surface. Second, the viewer's first encounter with the image is theirs to make sense of. We don't tell them what to feel. They figure it out. That moment of figuring-it-out is what creates ownership.

Consistency is the new persuasion. We don't argue. We recur.
§VIII — Banned

What this is not.

A negative-space list. If a frame leans in any of these directions, stop shooting and reset. None of these are subjective calls — they are the visual category default Signal exists to refuse.
  • The influencer pose. Hand on neck. Arched back. Lips parted. Half-smile to camera. Anything that reads "I am being photographed."
  • Studio white-wall portrait. Seamless paper. Daylight-balanced softbox. Catalog framing.
  • Gym selfie / gym mirror. Anything that frames the body as an optimization project.
  • AARP-coded "vital senior." Older subjects shown as inspirational specimens of aging well. Patronizing. Not what we do.
  • Before-and-after composition. Any structural promise of transformation.
  • Bottle in hand / product-in-hand. The subject holding the packet, the tray, the pendant up to the camera. That's a product shot. Different register.
  • Brand t-shirt or logo merch visible. No Signal-branded apparel in frame. Ever. (See §VI banned.)
  • Smiling-at-camera with perfect teeth. Performative warmth. Not ours.
  • Lifestyle props that don't decode. Generic mug, generic candle, generic plant. If a prop doesn't carry a sign, it's clutter.
  • Captions burned into the image. No headline overlay. No watermark. No "Signal." in the corner.
