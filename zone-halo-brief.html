2The one rule that governs every formulation call

Read this before the ingredient table — it explains why the doses look the way they do and where we'll need your regulatory discipline.

"We name the dose, not the hype."

Signal is cellcare — foundation-first, not outcome-first. That commits us to three things that directly shape your work:

• Real, clinically-evidenced doses — no fairy dust, no proprietary blends. Every active is named on the label at a dose that does real work. The ethos is minimum effective dose, maximum effect — enough to matter, nothing wasted (creatine being the deliberate gram-scale exception).
• No outcome claims. We describe what each ingredient does inside the cell, never what the customer will feel. This is our moat and our legal posture at once — and it's where your reg consultant earns his keep reviewing our copy.
• We are obsessive about the claims line. Example: we keep the word "inflammation" entirely off any label or consumer surface, on purpose. We'd rather under-claim than trip DSHEA. Hold us to this; it's a feature, not a constraint we resent.

You flagged that retailers and marketplaces now assay-test products against their labels. Good — that's the same discipline from the other side. Build in the overage so every active tests at or above label, and we'll treat third-party COAs as non-negotiable.

What's locked vs. what's open — read before §3–§4

Four product constraints are fixed by us — please formulate within them, not around them. Everything else (doses, ingredient swaps, the creatine-format mechanics) is yours to settle.

• Format: a chew. Not capsules, softgels, or a loose powder. The chew is the ritual, and the ritual is the moat.
• Single-serve: individually packaged. One sealed daily unit (the sachet) — not a tub or a bottle.
• Branded ingredients only. Every active is a named, clinically-studied branded form (Kaneka, NNB, Mitsubishi, Albion/Balchem) — never a generic or a commodity blend. It's the honesty position, and it scores on third-party rating rubrics (SuppCo, Fullscript QA).
• Taste is the priority — and it wins the tiebreaker. When taste conflicts with anything short of dose-honesty, taste wins (mechanics in §4). The one thing taste does not override is §2: we never quietly under-dose to chase flavor — we move mass out of the chew, or we trim a dose transparently.

4The decision the two days exist to settle — form factor

You already put your finger on this on the call. It's our number-one question — so we're bringing a proposed answer rather than a blank page, with the understanding that the two-day, in person, settles the final format.

Chew count per sachet, stated honestly

The product is a daily sachet holding however many chews the final formula needs — so the chew count is not fixed at one, two, or three. It falls out of the finished ingredient list, and that list is exactly what we're settling with you. Fewer is better — a Hi-Chew-style bite you look forward to, not a chore, since the ritual is part of the moat — but chew count is not the priority; taste is. Founder ruling: taste wins the tiebreaker. If a great-tasting chew and a full single-bite dose ever fight, taste wins — and we settle it by moving mass out of the chew (the creatine split below), not by quietly under-dosing. Dose-honesty from §2 still governs: any dose we trim, we trim out loud.

The physics — and the realization that it's really one ingredient, not six: you can get ~2 g of active into a Starburst-sized chew. Our load is roughly 5 g creatine + ~1.5 g magnesium bisglycinate (raw) + under 0.2 g for the four branded longevity actives combined ≈ ~6.7 g of raw active before excipients, flavor, and binder. Creatine alone is ~75% of that mass. Strip it out and the five milligram-scale "machinery" actives total ~1.7 g — comfortably 1–2 chews. So this isn't a six-ingredient packing problem; it's a creatine problem. To be precise about it, since you know the format cold: creatine can be chewed — 1 g creatine chews already ship — so 5 g isn't impossible, it's roughly five chews. The real question isn't whether creatine can ride in a chew, but whether 5 g of it should: five chews breaks the look-forward-to ritual, and gram-scale creatine in a soft, moist matrix raises the stability point you flagged (degradation to creatinine). So pulling creatine into its own format is a deliberate choice, not a workaround for an impossibility.

That reframe matters because creatine is a gram-scale fuel reserve — a different kind of thing than the five milligram-scale machinery actives. Our own Cellular Framework already names that seam: five jobs of a working cell, plus the Reserve. Physics and concept point at the same line, which drives our proposed answer:

Our proposed resolution — for you to pressure-test

One product, one ritual: a daily sachet = ≤2 "foundation" chews (the five mg-scale actives) + creatine in a format that holds grams — a mix-in stick or a creatine-specific mini-tab in the same sachet. Keeps the single product and the ≤2-chew ritual you look forward to, and it's honest to the fact that creatine is the Reserve, not the machinery.

Alternatives we weighed, for you to weigh against it:

  1. Trim the creatine dose (5 g → 3 g — you noted studies run 2–5 g; 3 g saturates over ~4 weeks). We'd only do this transparently — a name-the-dose brand can't quietly under-dose.
  2. Cut redundancy / tighten doses on the other five (section 5) to reclaim chew space.
  3. Split creatine into its own SKU — foundation sachet + a separate creatine unit. Cleanest formula-wise, but it reopens the two-product path and its cost/marketing weight. Our fallback if a mixed-format sachet isn't practical.
  4. Accept ~5 all-in-one chews (creatine can be chewed — 1 g chews already exist — so 5 g is about five chews) and make them good enough to stay a ritual. Effectively ruled out by the taste-wins ruling — five chews is a mouthful, not a treat, and gram-scale creatine in a soft, moist matrix raises texture and stability concerns. Listed for completeness, not as a live option.

The final format is settled with you in the two-day — this is our starting proposal, not a fixed spec. The one operational answer we most need from you: can your chew manufacturer produce a mixed-format sachet (chews + a powder stick or tab in one sealed unit) at a sane cost? If that's impractical, we fall back to splitting creatine into its own SKU.

The disruptor question, answered honestly

Signal is not a disruptor on thesis. "Foundation before outcomes" feels like white space from the inside, but the language is already owned by brands far bigger than us — Seed calls supplements "amplifiers," AG1 owns "foundational daily nutrition," CellCore sells "foundation first, then outcomes." If we lead with the thesis, we sound derivative.

The disruption, if there is one, is execution — not idea.

The one slot in this category that is genuinely empty is ours by design: a premium, branded-form, honesty-disciplined cellular product in a daily chew people actually look forward to. Every serious player — Timeline, Qualia, Wonderfeel, NOVOS — is a capsule, softgel, or powder. None is a treat. So our odds of being a real disruptor ride on one variable, and it's the one you're here to settle: can the chew carry this formula at a count people enjoy? If yes, the niche is ours and it's defensible on ritual. If it takes four chalky chews, the moat evaporates and we're a premium capsule brand fighting clinical walls we can't out-spend. Section 4 isn't a packaging detail — it's the whole disruptor thesis.

Three competitors define the ground we're standing on — only one of them shows up in an ingredient overlap search:

Timeline / Mitopure — the threat that shares zero ingredients with us

Urolithin A, single hero molecule, $50M+ R&D, 25 human trials, 80+ patents, Nestlé Health Science + L'Oréal backing. They own the exact sentence our PQQ and spermidine nodes whisper — "clear out damaged mitochondria, build new ones, = cellular energy." Their version is loud and clinically walled; ours is quiet and mechanistic. This is who to benchmark against, not IM8. The formulation question it raises for you: do we need our own substantiated mitophagy/biogenesis story, or do we stay disciplined and let them have that claim while we win on the floor underneath it?

The NAD blind spot — this one is yours to settle, Jake

We built the one product in this space with no NAD precursor (no NMN, no NR) and no mitophagy hero. Every Tier 1–2 competitor anchors on NMN, NR, or urolithin A — the market has trained the customer to ask "where's your NAD story?" Our thesis answer is deliberate: protect and fuel the mitochondria you already have, don't just floor the NAD gas pedal. But the regulatory excuse for skipping it is gone — FDA reversed the NMN exclusion in Sept/Dec 2025, so NMN is a dietary-supplement-legal ingredient again (NDI notification still required). That turns "no NAD" from a compliance default into a pure formulation choice. We want your hand on exactly this: is a no-NAD cellular formula a defensible wedge, or a hole a sophisticated buyer will see straight through? If it belongs in, it changes the chew load and the stability picture (NMN degrades in moisture — a chew is close to its worst environment).
