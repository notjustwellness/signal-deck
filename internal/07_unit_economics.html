What it costs, what it returns

A pre-launch estimate, anchored by real ingredient costs — validated at Zone Halo.

Read this first

Signal is pre-launch. Every figure here is a bottom-up estimate, anchored by three real ingredient costs (Kaneka QH® ubiquinol, MitoPrime® at $980/kg, creatine at $11/kg) — not yet a validated quote. The hero is shown as a range, not a single point. Locking the hard cost stack is the explicit first use of seed proceeds.

Two things to hold: the cost estimate validates against the trimmed working set (doc 02) and final doses at the Zone Halo two-day; and the second SKU is the near-nil-margin pure-creatine trust SKU, not a margin product. This doc ladders to the Foundation Brief; if anything here contradicts it, the brief wins.

Chapter 01

The honest headline

Chews on branded actives are structurally costlier than capsules — yet the hero clears a healthy margin, because the premium actives are milligram-dosed and cost little.
  • The hero clears a healthy margin. The bottom-up estimate lands at roughly ~70–79% gross margin at $89 (plan ~75%), because the costly actives are dosed in milligrams and the formula carries no gram-scale cost line (spermidine is the milligram-dosed NNB Puremidine® form).
  • Two metrics, kept distinct. Gross margin = revenue − COGS (~75%). Contribution margin = revenue − all per-unit variable cost incl. fulfillment, parcel, payment (~60%). This is contribution before marketing — don't compare it to outside "contribution margin" figures that already subtract ad spend; those sit a layer lower. CAC is paid out of this ~60% — but it can't all go to acquisition: what's left after CAC still has to cover fixed overhead (salaries, software, rent) and profit. And one order's margin isn't the verdict on the business — that's LTV:CAC over a year (Ch 04).
  • Still an estimate — and two things to confirm. Three real anchors are in hand; the rest of the actives, overage, and yield are not yet quoted (the widest line is the Kaneka QH® ubiquinol grade — its real quote sets the range). Two checks decide whether the 75% is real: COGS must be landed — inbound freight, duties, and shrink belong in COGS, not below the line — and 75% is the list-price margin; the realized margin after the subscription discount runs lower.

Chapter 03

The creatine trust SKU — near-nil margin by design

This is not a profit line, and that is deliberate. The old "$49 daily-fuel SKU" economics are retired; the second SKU is now the pure-creatine tin.
  • Priced near cost, on purpose. 5 g NNB creatine in a single-use tin, sold well below the marked-up single-serve competition — low enough to be a no-brainer. Margin is ~nil by design; we price it "not to lose money," not to make it.
  • The return is not revenue. It's trust + retention + basket: a clean, pure ingredient fused into the flagship's daily ritual, sold bundle-only (never standalone). It deepens the habit that drives flagship re-order — the metric that actually carries LTV.
  • Why it's still worth shipping. A near-cost companion that reinforces the daily ritual and demonstrates radical honesty (we make no money here) buys more lifetime value through the hero than it forgoes in its own margin.

Re-order is the LTV metric. For an imperceptible product, a re-order miss is as likely a conviction/mechanic problem as a product one — so the founding cohort is instrumented to tell those apart before we scale spend (doc 04, doc 08).

The model — subscription-first, with an honest off switch Locked

Subscription-first — 15% off list, cancel anytime. Monthly auto-replenish is the default at 15% off ($89 → ~$75.65), and it matches the daily-practice thesis. Framed as the 90-day starter — because a cellular-foundation product works on a ~90-day timescale, not a day-one buzz, so we ask people to give it the time it actually needs. The 15% is a genuine pass-through for the recurring relationship, not a standing markdown that turns $89 into a fiction (that would break the price-honesty rule, doc 08), and it carries no lock-in — the 90 days is a recommendation, not a contract. The recurring relationship is the engine.

But the control is a simple on/off switch. Pausing or stopping is one tap — no retention maze, no "call to cancel," no dark pattern. This is the effortlessness wedge (doc 04) and the relay rule applied to billing: we never trap the person a voucher sent us.

Why it's an asset, not a leak. A frictionless off switch raises measured churn versus a trap — and that's the point. It keeps re-order an honest signal: someone who switches off is telling us the product isn't earning its place, not that cancelling was too hard. Trapped revenue is fake LTV that hides a conviction problem; we'd rather see the truth and fix the product. Honest retention compounds; trapped retention rots.

And the commercial edge, not just the hygienic one. Feeling trapped by the subscription is the single largest churn driver in the category (~30% of cancels — doc 09's churn map). So the honest switch isn't only measurement integrity; it's a direct play on the biggest leak: the brands that build the maze bleed their largest cohort to resentment — the one-tap switch is how we turn that reason-to-quit into a reason-to-trust. (The ~30% weight is one external source, treated as a prior to confirm with the founding cohort — doc 09.)

Chapter 05

Margin levers

How the margin protects and improves — three real levers, none of them a gimmick.
  • Cost came in low. The milligram-dosed premium actives and the Puremidine® swap are why the hero clears ~70–79% gross. Validation turns the estimate into fact and unblocks manufacturing — but it confirms the model's strongest number, not its riskiest. The event that actually proves the business is the retention readout (Ch 04, Ch 07), and capital can't make that resolve faster than calendar time.
  • Price headroom. $89 is the floor; the premium position supports $99–120 (AG1 sits at ~$99). Any price move up is margin upside on top of the stack above.
  • MOQ scale. Moving order volume from 10K to 25K–50K units pulls per-unit cost down ~20–40%. Scale is a margin lever we earn by selling through.

Paid is a cap, not an engine — narrowly aimed at the already-converted, never cold prospecting. This is the central growth risk, not a watch item. Warm channels (founder reach, seeded tribe, peer sampling, practitioner referral) are high-trust but low-volume, and they don't scale linearly — and the Fullscript path means competing for shelf-of-mind against entrenched professional lines (Thorne, Pure Encapsulations, Designs for Health) while sharing margin with the channel. The ~$35 paid cap sits below the category's "strong" CAC floor, achievable only if relay genuinely carries the volume. That's fine for the founding cohort and the central unknown for the round after this one — a model that refuses cold prospecting has an unknown, possibly low, ceiling you won't see until you hit it. Explicit cohort-phase experiment: what is relay's realistic monthly volume ceiling, and what's the marginal CAC above it?

Chapter 07

The raise & use of funds

A small seed, two jobs: validate the cost stack (fast, cheap, unblocks manufacturing) and fund runway long enough to read the founding cohort's retention — the number that actually proves the model.
  • Raise: $800K – $1M (seed). Sized to the model — not an $8M round; you don't need that to manufacture a dense founding cohort, run a city-by-city beachhead, and fund runway through the retention readout (~9–12 mo) with buffer. The binding constraint on the raise isn't building the cohort; it's surviving long enough to read the curve before the next round.
  • First use of funds — lock the cost stack (sequencing, not headline). A defined portion engages Zone Halo to convert the bottom-up estimate into a hard, validated cost stack and a launch-ready formula within 90 days of close. Correct to do first — it's cheap, fast, and unblocks manufacturing — and every downstream figure (margin, LTV, follow-on math) locks the moment it lands. But it confirms the model's strongest number; it is not the event that proves the business.
  • The headline de-risking milestone — the retention readout. What the next investor will demand is proof of retained cycles, and that is calendar-bound: no seed makes it resolve faster than time. The leading indicator — the 60–90-day early-repeat rate — is the first real read; the full ~6-cycle curve takes 9–12 months (early churn is front-loaded). The trap to avoid: anchoring on the 90-day "formula locked" win and under-funding the 9–12-month window that proves the model. If retention lands at the bear case, we'd rather learn it on our own runway than discover it after the seed is spent.
  • The Zone Halo deal terms. The engagement is a $30K Option B fee plus a 12-month retainer (fixed costs, funded here) and a ~$0.67/unit royalty (the per-unit line in the §2 cost stack). The royalty is small enough to sit inside the margin; the fee + retainer are the first-use-of-funds spend that buys the validated formula.
  • Then — manufacture the MCM. "Build the un-revocable core first": the founding cohort does triple duty — day-one social proof, proof of retention before $1 of paid, and the seed of an owned community. Prime the engine before launch — don't hope it ignites after.
Chapter 08

What we refuse — and what we don't know yet

The bright lines on disclosure, and the open figures the raise closes.
  • We never publish the margin. On an $89 product, "here's our margin" reads as "you're paying for $22 of stuff" — it invites "why so greedy." The honest external number is ingredient spend — "money in the bottle" — what we pay for a real dose of each branded active, a figure a hype competitor can't match without exposing its own underdosing. Margin is an internal number; spend is the public one.
  • No margin theatre, either direction. We don't brag about COGS savings and we don't manufacture a "premium" cost story. The price is justified by the dose, not by a markup narrative.
