How Signal looks, speaks & claims

The wordmark, the voice, and the claims law that governs what we may say.

This is the rulebook for everything outward-facing — the wordmark, the voice, and the claims law that governs what we may say on a label, a page, or an ad. When in doubt, this doc is what a writer, designer, or partner checks first.

How to read this

It ladders to the Foundation Brief — if anything here contradicts the brief, the brief wins. The single rule that governs the rest: "name the dose, not the hype."

Chapter 01

Naming & architecture

Get the names right first — they're the most-repeated thing we own.
  • Wellness — the parent company, "the cellcare company." INSDR is always the logo, never typed as a word; the following word Wellness is always set in Bebas Neue.
  • cellcare — the category we focus on: not another outcome on the shelf, but the floor underneath all of them. Lowercase, one word.
  • Signal — the first sub-brand under the parent with a product to sell. More sub-brands may follow; Signal is where it starts. This is the consumer-facing name.

Written hierarchy: INSDR Wellness (company) → cellcare (category) → Signal (brand) → the two products (flagship + creatine companion). Never collapse "Signal" and "INSDR" into one name; never present the creatine SKU as a second brand.

Chapter 03

The wordmark Locked

The mark at the top of this page is the canonical lockup. Don't redraw it.

The canonical wordmark, full size — bone letters, red brackets, shown in the espresso chip (the on-light-surface treatment).

  • Form: the word signal set as a typographic phonetic spelling — /sɪgnɑːl/ — in a traced serif, wrapped in red brackets. The brackets are the brand device (the "signal" being framed).
  • Colour: bone letters + red brackets. On a light surface, the mark sits in an espresso chip (the dark rounded lockup). On a dark surface, no chip — bone/red directly on the ground.
  • Parent mark: INSDR always appears as its logo image, never typed. It sits small, secondary to Signal.
  • Don't: recolour the brackets, set the wordmark in a normal (non-phonetic) spelling as a logo, stretch, or add effects. The single source of truth is the SVG reused across these docs.
Chapter 04

Voice & tone

Signal sounds like a knowledgeable friend who refuses to oversell you — not a brand, not a lab, not a hype machine.
We doWe don't
Speak plainly; lowercase tagline; short sentencesWarm-up fluff, exclamation hype, ALL-CAPS shouting
Name the dose and the branded form (receipts)Vague superlatives ("world's best," "clinically proven" with no cite)
Lead with what's true, including what not to expectPromise feelings or outcomes we can't substantiate
Be product-led — inform, let the proof vouchBeg, nag, or guilt the reader into adherence
Credit sources honestly (suppliers, the formulator)Borrow authority we can't name or stand behind
Frame every rival tactic as distraction from the dose — answer hype and a personalization quiz the same wayPick a new villain for each competitor

The relay test for any sentence: would it embarrass the person who recommended us if they read it aloud? If yes, cut it. Our copy's job is to arm the voucher, not to close the sale by force.

Chapter 05

The claims law Locked

The big one. Ban the hype, not the benefit. Substantiated benefits are allowed — hype verbs and disease claims are not.

A real, hedged structure/function benefit is permitted when it's paired with the named dose (the receipt). What's banned is the inflation: hype verbs, fabricated feelings, and anything that strays toward a disease claim. Two gates clear every claim before it ships.

AllowedBanned
Hedged S/F benefit + dose: "supports cellular energy" "helps maintain healthy magnesium status"Hype verbs: boosts · improves · transforms · optimizes · unlocks · skyrockets
Verbs: supports · helps maintain · plays a role in · contributes toOutcome theater: "take X, get result Y," before/after promises
Cited mechanism at our actual dose, with the branded form namedFabricated read-out: narrating a feeling the user is "supposed" to have
Honest felt language: "no instant hit — builds quietly"Disease claims: treats / prevents / cures / reduces inflammation — an illegal wall

The two gates — every claim clears both

1 · Substantiation. The claim must be true at our dose — supported by evidence for the branded form at the amount we actually use, not a study at 5× the dose.

2 · Zone Halo reg sign-off. Every public claim is reviewed for DSHEA / FTC compliance before it goes live. No claim ships on a writer's confidence alone.

The inflammation rule — non-negotiable
  • "Inflammation" is a disease-claim trigger and never appears on a public or ungated surface. The founder's general reasoning about bodies may discuss it in gated internal docs only — it must be stripped before any public surface and routed through Zone Halo reg review. Signal never claims to reduce inflammation; the word does not migrate onto the label.
Chapter 06

Taglines & lines

One tagline rules. Two lines are locked; the legacy lines below have been ruled out — do not revive them.
  • "send the right signal." Locked — the one brand tagline, lowercase, above everything.
  • "name the dose, not the hype." Locked — the method line / claims-law slogan. Internal-and-external safe.
Campaign line — situational, not a tagline
  • When. Deploy only when the conversation is about personalization; it resolves on the locked slogan and retires after. Pre-launch use "detours"; graduate to "theater" once we've earned the standing.
  • The line. "There are two ways to avoid telling you how much is actually in the bottle. You can bury it under big promises, or you can bury it under personalization — the quiz, the kit, the monitor. Both are detours. Both end at the same boring fundamentals. We just started there, and put the number on the label. Name the dose, not the hype."
Legacy lines — retired (ruled, 2026-06-23)
  • "the signal your cells are waiting for" → OUT. Leans promise-y and personifies the cells. Not a standing line; at most an occasional headline, never canon.
  • "Foundation first. Everything stacks." → RETIRED. "Everything stacks" is from the old stacking generation and fights the minimalism spine ("minimum dosage, maximum provable effect").
  • "candy-first" → RETIRED as a public line. It undercuts the serious-health positioning; "taste is the priority" already carries the idea without the word.
Chapter 07

Making the proof legible

The honesty position is subtle; the loud-but-blunt competitor is not. The brand's job is to make our proof impossible to miss — without becoming hype.
  • Show, ranked. The proof record (doc 05) is surfaced as a skimmable, ranked thing — external proof first — not buried in fine print. Legible beats true-but-quiet.
  • Numbers, not adjectives. "Every lot, third-party tested, results published" beats "the highest-quality ingredients." Specifics read as proof; adjectives read as marketing.
  • Restraint is the flex. Telling people what not to expect is itself a headline. Honesty, stated plainly and prominently, is the loudest thing we can legally say.
  • Precision is the value story — make it legible. A short ingredient list loses to a kitchen-sink label unless we say why: every active does a specific job in cellular communication → energy, with little-to-no overlap, and we tell the customer what each one is for. "Fewer because each one is load-bearing" must be stated as prominently as a competitor's long ingredient deck — otherwise minimalism reads as less value (product logic in doc 02).

Chapter 08

What we refuse Locked

The clearest way to know what Signal is, is to know what it refuses.
  • No outcome theater — no pill-X-gives-result-Y promises, no hype verbs, no disease claims.
  • No felt-effect gimmick — no stimulant or drug bolted on to manufacture a buzz (paraxanthine and NMN-as-mirror rejected). The felt layer is honest or it isn't there.
  • No quiet under-dosing — taste never wins over dose-honesty; we disclose every trim.
  • No fabricated read-out — we never narrate a feeling the user is "supposed" to have.
  • Never use the man — the founder's personal wound never becomes ad copy; only the impersonal mechanism flows downhill into marketing.
  • No greenwashing — no "recyclable" / "sustainable" claims we can't stand behind.
  • No vague authority — no "world's best." Sean Wells is used only as a "formulated by" credential; if a name can't be used honestly, we drop it rather than dress it up.
  • No second hero — the trust SKU and any future product stay companions; one flagship.

Founder visibility: Damon is the known, attributable founder — but not the day-to-day public face (anonymous-and-authentic is a contradiction). His lack of a formulation background is an honesty asset: he's the customer's proxy, not the lab coat.
