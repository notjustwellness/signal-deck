The channel is people

Who we sell to, in what order — and why trust travels by relay, not by ads.

We do not buy our way in. A trust brand can't out-spend the category on ads without becoming the thing it refuses to be. The channel is relay: trust flows through people. Our job is to equip the person who vouches for us — not to persuade a cold buyer.

So the audience is sequenced, not blended: credibility seeds make us believable, one dense audience pays the bills, and scale comes last — only after re-order proof. This doc ladders to Foundation Brief §06 (who it's for) and §05 (the relay rule); if anything here contradicts the brief, the brief wins. Channel-partner mechanics live in doc 05 — here we cover audience, sequence, and the relay engine.

Chapter 01

The GTM thesis — relay, not reach

A trust brand can't win by out-shouting the category. The mechanism has to match the message.
  • The channel is people. Trust flows through a person who already has it — a colleague, a coach, a clinician, a respected skeptic. We don't try to convert a cold stranger; we arm the voucher and let them carry us the last foot.
  • Why not paid acquisition first. Performance ads reward the loudest claim and the biggest budget — the exact game an honesty brand loses by playing. Paid has a role later (retargeting warm traffic, not cold prospecting), but it is never the engine.
  • The relay rule. Our north star is to never embarrass the person who recommended us. Every GTM tactic is judged against it: if a move could make a voucher look foolish, it's off the table.
  • Trust starts borrowed. Before we've earned our own, we borrow it — from the people who vouch, the rubrics that score us, and the discipline of inviting a teardown ("tear it apart") rather than asking for faith.

The anti-gimmick test, applied to GTM

For any growth tactic: "would a dishonest competitor do this too?" Buying influencer vouches, gating reviews, juicing scarcity — all things a hype brand does. If the tactic survives only because it's loud, it's marketing, not relay. We grow on equipped advocates and earned proof, or we grow slower.

Honesty doesn't sell — it qualifies and keeps

The correction that disciplines this whole doc: research refuted the idea that premium brands "compete primarily on trust." People buy on product attributes — dose, price, format, aesthetic — and on the voucher's credibility. Trust's job in growth is narrower and real: it grants permission to be considered (it clears the skeptic's gate) and it retains. It is not the thing that closes the sale. So acquisition leans on the relay + the concrete product; honesty is what makes the voucher safe to give and the customer slow to leave — never the pitch that converts a cold stranger. Pointing the proof record at acquisition and expecting it to sell is the trap; aim it at permission and retention.

The shape: two credibility seeds (responders = trust, biohacker = data) make us believable → one dense revenue audience (veteran/tactical) pays the bills and lights word-of-mouth → the cabinet-skeptic converts behind the hook → longevity/affluent is the scale tier, last. One door — the cabinet — both the emotional buyer and the skeptic walk through; after it the stories fork and never blend.

Who "veteran / tactical-fitness" actually means

It is not only military veterans. It's the broader tactical-athlete world: current and former military, plus the civilian culture that trains the way soldiers, police, and firefighters train — functional strength and work-capacity for the job, not the mirror. Rucking, strength-endurance, conditioning built for real-world readiness rather than aesthetics.

Where they live: tactical-fitness programs and gyms, veteran-owned training brands and apparel, rucking and work-capacity communities (load-carriage events, minimalist strength-endurance programming, mountain-athlete training), and the unit gyms, ranges, and online forums where these people coach each other. Tight networks where a recommendation travels fast and sticks.

What defines them culturally: discipline over hype, hard-earned skepticism of "snake oil," and fierce loyalty to gear that proves itself. They spend on readiness — supplements are a normal part of training budget, not an indulgence — and they want the floor under them to be solid, which is exactly Signal's "foundation, not a burst."

The line vs. the first-responder seed (§2 above): overlapping tribe, different job. First responders are the credibility seed — we earn their trust to be believable, not to bill them. Veteran/tactical-fitness is the revenue engine — the same values, but a population that buys performance-foundation products as routine training spend. Many people sit in both; we treat them by the role we're asking them to play, never blend the two.

One concrete consequence: a meaningful share are drug-tested or train alongside people who are — so NSF Certified for Sport is a real purchase driver for them, not a nicety. That's a direct reason the cert decision in doc 05/06 isn't optional polish; it's table stakes for the audience that pays.

Chapter 03

Why these — and why not the obvious ones

With no Signal-specific trial yet, the deciding question isn't "who loves the science." It's who buys on belief vs who demands proof — and whether the credibility halo transfers.
  • Veteran / tactical-fitness wins revenue because they're belief-buyers (they'll commit before a trial exists), their identity is dense (recommendations travel inside a tight tribe), and the first-responder halo transfers perfectly — it's the same world. Lowest capture risk of the belief-buying audiences.
  • Biohackers are a trap as revenue — counterintuitive, because the brand fits them. They demand proof and will stall on "no trial yet"; loyalty is near zero (they rotate to the next protocol, torching the re-order gate); and the halo doesn't transfer — they trust data, not heroes. So they're a second credibility seed, not the engine.
  • CrossFit / functional-fitness was the tempting #2 — highest density, fastest ignition — but it carries the strongest pull back toward "performance / energy-drink," the exact box we killed. We chose veteran/tactical to protect "foundation, not a burst." The velocity-vs-integrity tension is real and worth revisiting if growth stalls (see flags).
  • Longevity-affluent is the buyer persona, not a seed — they pay and fit, but they're diffuse and low-density, so they can't ignite word-of-mouth cold. They're the scale tier, earned after re-order proof.
Chapter 04

The relay engine — how trust actually moves

Relay is a system, not a vibe. It has roles, rules, and ammunition — and one mechanic, built into the product, that turns a customer into a voucher.

Send a Signal — the relay, built into every purchase Locked

Every purchase carries the gift. Baked into checkout, the buyer can send a Signal — a one-time sample — to a friend or loved one. It's not a coupon or a refer-a-friend bolt-on; it's the relay made physical: the moment a customer becomes a voucher, in one tap.

Why it's the engine, not a feature. The sample arrives from a person, not from us — peer-initiated trust, the only kind that converts in a low-trust category. It's a trial that doesn't betray the no-hype stance, because a friend chose to send it; we never bought the introduction. The name itself carries the brand line ("send the right signal").

Why a one-time sample, not a discount. A sample lets the product earn its own verdict — taste, ritual, the honest "what to expect" — at low gift COGS and near-zero CAC (doc 07). A discount buys a transaction; a sample earns a believer.

The voucher — the person who carries us

The respected individual inside a tribe (a department's fittest medic, a veteran coach, a label-reading skeptic others defer to). We don't pay them. We equip them — with the open formula, the COA, the "what not to expect" honesty — so vouching for us is safe and makes them look smart. Naming the keystone voucher per seed is an open item (see flags).

The founder — known, not the face

Damon stays the known founder (anonymous + authentic is a contradiction) but is not the public spokesperson. His lack of a formulation background is an authenticity asset, not a liability — he's the man at the cabinet, not the chemist on a pedestal. The story uses the mechanism, never the man.

The credential vs the vouch

A formulator (e.g. Sean Wells) is a "formulated by" credential — not an endorsement. If a name can't be used, we drop it; we never paper over it with a vague "world's best." Credentials sit on the product; vouches come from people who chose us freely.

The daily voice — arms the relay, doesn't replace it

Our owned content is a product-led backbone that informs — it hands the voucher better language and the buyer better understanding. It does not vouch for itself (a brand can't be its own third party). Content is ammunition for the relay layer, not a substitute for it.

Chapter 05

Institutional relay — rubrics & clinician channels

The same relay logic, scaled through institutions that lend us trust we can't assert ourselves. Full mechanics live in doc 05; here's the GTM read.
  • SuppCo (now Function) — the badge that becomes a channel. An independent rubric score isn't just a badge on our page; because Function acquired SuppCo, a good score is distribution into a 50M+ test audience that already trusts the rubric. We formulate to the rubric pre-launch so the score is earned, not scraped.
  • FrontrowMD / Fullscript — borrowed clinician authority. A clinician recommendation is third-party trust we could never claim ourselves. Warm these after label-lock + COA.
  • The sequencing trap (honesty-brand-specific). A failed cert or a mediocre score is permanent reputational damage for us — far worse than for a normal brand, because transparency is the pitch. Rule: earn the rating only when the label is certainly dead-accurate (post-COA, supply stable). Never submit for hype.
A fork to hold open — not close early
  • Fullscript = DTC-vs-practitioner, not a checkbox. Listing in a practitioner dispensary changes margin (wholesale eats the $89 gross), positioning (clinical dispensary pulls against the consumer brand), and qualification (does the formula pass their QA?). Open the conversation pre-launch (cheap, de-risks formulation); treat listing as a Phase-2+ decision after DTC re-order proof. Don't let "add a channel" quietly become "become a practitioner brand."

MCM = Minimum Critical Mass: the smallest number of active, repeat, visible paying customers whose word-of-mouth becomes self-sustaining. We don't chase a big number first; we chase a dense one. Density — not reach — is what makes a relay ignite.

The beachhead — where we earn in, and where we hold

A hybrid of credibility and saturability, run as one funnel. The seed and the cohort are manufactured across two linked venues — one rented for reach, one owned for retention.

Rented top-of-funnel — earn-in credibility. A community like r/Supplements (~100k label-readers): we earn standing with founder-led teardown / POV content and never market at them. The reach is rented; the trust has to be earned, which is exactly the relay discipline.

Owned core — the saturable cohort engine. An owned Discord / Skool community where the founding cohort converts, sticks, and generates proof — part of the un-revocable owned core, the place the relay compounds and the MCM is held.

The founder content sprint is the on-ramp to both — it informs the rented audience and seeds the owned one. (Content arms the relay; it doesn't vouch — consistent with §4.)

Chapter 07

The GLP-1 tailwind — ride it, never become it

The biggest demand wave in the category is adjacent to us. We position beside it without letting it capture the brand.
  • The opening. People eating far less on GLP-1 medications still need cellular foundation — "feed the machinery when you're eating less" is a credible, on-thesis adjacency, and the under-fed-cell angle is relatively open ground.
  • The discipline. Our creatine is an anchor, not the differentiator — the GLP-1 + muscle-loss story is already crowded (big retail and brands are there). We reference the tailwind; we don't rebuild the brand around it.
  • The trap. Chasing outcome-capture ("the GLP-1 companion") would re-import the felt-effect / weight-loss promise we deliberately refuse. Ride it, never become it.
Chapter 08

The personalization wave — ride the desire, refuse the mechanism

A ~$15B tailwind is pulling the category toward quizzes, swabs and sensors. We take what people actually want from it and refuse the apparatus.
  • The opening. People don't want a swab — they want relevance, progress, and proof it's working. We deliver that payload with knowledge and ritual (the dose-response timeline, the daily voice), not hardware. "Made for you," without a lab.
  • The discipline. Personalization is selection, not formulation — the quiz picks which generic SKUs land in your box; almost no one changes the per-person dose. And the most data-driven version (ZOE-grade biology) collapses to the same fundamentals anyway. We skipped to the fundamentals and dosed them properly.
  • The trap. Bolting on a quiz, DNA kit or CGM would build the exact retention maze our churn data names as the #1 driver of quitting (doc 09) — and import a diagnostic claim surface we can't substantiate. Ride the desire, never become the apparatus.
What we can / can't say about personalization
  • Defensible — "most personalization changes which products you're sold, not the dose"; "even the most data-driven methods point back to the fundamentals." Backed by a graded systematic review of DNA-diet RCTs (13 of 15 conclusions: no effect) and the ZOE trial critique (its own output collapses to common-sense advice).
  • Off-limits — refuted by the research, never say — "personalization is proven worthless," "DNA diets don't work," "personalization has no clinical support." Each overreaches the evidence and is factually unsafe.
  • The rule — attack the practice, never the customer who bought one.

Chapter 09

What we refuse in growth

The bright lines the funnel is built inside.
  • No bought vouches. Paid endorsements read as exactly what they are; a tribe smells them and the rejection backfires. Vouches are earned through authentic adoption or they don't count.
  • No capture for velocity. We won't adopt energy-drink / performance-burst language to ignite a faster audience. Speed bought by betraying "foundation, not a burst" is a loss disguised as a win.
  • No personalization theater. We won't ship a quiz, DNA kit, microbiome test or CGM integration to simulate customization. The honest version of "made for you" is one rigorous formula, dosed to the research, that you fit to your day (the argument lives in §08).
  • No claims-creep at the funnel's edge. GTM copy lives under the same claims law as everything else (doc 03): "they trust the floor" is safe; "Signal keeps them safe on shift" is an illegal structure/function claim near a lives-on-the-line job. The funnel never says what the label can't.
  • No fake scarcity or gated proof. The waitlist is a real tribe, not a manufactured line; reviews and COAs are open, never curated to flatter. If a tactic only works because it's loud, it fails the relay rule.
  • The "thanks" trap. Give-back to a seed tribe (e.g. first responders) must be real, never a credibility prop. The founder blind spot is doing it for the thanks — the tribe resents a performance.
Open — decisions & build items
  • Name the keystone voucher — per seed (one respected first responder; one trusted skeptic-stacker). The relay can't start until a real person agrees to carry us.
  • The velocity-vs-integrity fork — veteran/tactical is chosen, but if growth stalls, revisit CrossFit/functional-fitness. Settling question: can Signal win that velocity without adopting that language?
  • First-responder access model — they're a trust seed, not revenue; the give-back / pricing model for $89-on-public-pay is undefined and must be real, not optics.
  • The trial gap shapes sequencing — until a Signal-specific study exists, proof-demanding audiences (biohackers) stay seeds, not revenue. Effect proof is borrowed + re-order data.
  • Fullscript DTC-vs-practitioner fork — Phase-2+ decision after DTC re-order proof (see §5).
