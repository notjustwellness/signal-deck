Before you read it as a funnel

This is not one funnel with one persona. Signal has two people who both walk this path, and blending them loses both — the exact "we lose both rooms by aiming at the gap between them" trap. So the map runs two lanes through the same four stages. The Foundation-Builder is who buys (warm, emotional, maintenance-minded). The Stacker is who validates (cold, skeptical, will pick apart your doses and your missing trial). They share one door — the cabinet — then they fork. Read each lane top to bottom as one person's whole path.

Awareness is the cheapest stage. Retention is the only one that decides whether the company lives.

The four stages aren't equal. The whole map is engineered toward a single number at the end: re-order ≥40%. That is the real gate — not reviews, not traffic, not a launch date. Everything upstream exists to make a stranger believe; the re-order rate is the only thing that proves they were right to.

One reframe you asked for, corrected: you can't align the message to "core health outcomes" — promising an outcome breaks U.S. supplement law (DSHEA) and our own moat ("we name the dose, not the outcome"). So this map aligns the foundation message to the felt job each segment is hiring Signal for — quiet control, vindication — never to a clinical result. The outcome language stays sealed. See §5.

·The shape — one door, two lanes

Everyone enters through the same emotional door (the medicine cabinet, the quiet question). After that one shared moment, the stories fork and never blend again. Cold traffic — a stranger scrolling — only ever stands at the doormat; the tagline is all they get until they self-select into a lane.

The one shared door · all lanes enter here

"You take care of yourself. Can you actually tell what's holding the whole system up?" The cabinet full of bottles. The quiet fear of where the family tree points. This moment works on everyone — the buyer and the skeptic both feel it. Cold traffic sees only the tagline at this door: "send the right signal."

Lane A · the buyer

The Foundation-Builder

Healthy, 30–55, already invests in their body, quietly afraid of inheriting the family tree. Hires Signal for quiet control — to stop chasing and start maintaining. Buys on trust + ritual. This lane is the revenue.

Lane B · the validator

The Stacker / Optimizer

Takes everything, reads three studies first, low loyalty, high scrutiny. Hires Signal for vindication — a brand that finally respects the homework. We don't hunt them; we win them once curious. Won once, they vouch — and their vouch converts Lane A.

The lanes are not independent. Lane B is an accelerant for Lane A — the skeptic's public "even I'm convinced" is the single most powerful trust marker the Foundation-Builder will ever see. That's why Phase 1 recruits the founding cohort from Lane B even though Lane A is who we ultimately sell to (see GTM strategy §02).

5Aligning the foundation message to the segment — the legal-and-moat-safe way

You asked to align messaging with the core health outcomes of each segment. Direct answer: don't — at least not in the words. Naming a health outcome ("more energy," "sharper focus," "prevents decline") is a disease/structure-function claim that needs FTC/FDA-grade substantiation we don't have, and it collapses the one thing that makes us different. So we align to the felt job — the end-state the customer is privately hiring us for — and let the science travel only as far as the dose. The outcome lives in the customer's head; we never put it in our mouth.

If the map does one thing

Engineer the whole path backward from re-order ≥40%

Awareness and Conversion are the stages every competitor optimizes — and they're the cheap ones. Trust-Building is where we win (the category skips it), and Retention is where we either have a company or don't. Every content pillar and trust marker above earns its place only if it moves a real customer toward the second box. A journey that converts strangers but doesn't retain them isn't a funnel — it's a way to spend money faster.

So the discipline: when choosing what to build or post, ask "does this make the 40% more likely?" If it only lifts awareness or first-purchase, it's secondary. The founding cohort isn't a marketing tactic — it's the instrument that measures whether this whole map is real.

Fences & open questions — read before this goes near a customer
  • DSHEA fence: nothing in the "outcome they privately want" column ever becomes copy. That column is a planning aid, not a script. The inflammation line and the sealed founder story stay off all cold/public surfaces until Zone Halo legal review.
  • UGC = FTC liability: every founding-cohort review must disclose the free-product connection and use "practice-and-feel" prompts. A review that implies cure/disease gets blamed on us. Build auto-disclosure into the Phase 0 review system.
  • The 40% number is the working floor, not gospel guessing — it's a placeholder for "motivated founders re-order at a rate that predicts strangers will." Pressure-test the exact threshold against real subscription-value math once Phase 1 has data; don't treat 40% as sacred.
  • Lane B is a dependency, not a guarantee: the whole map assumes we can earn standing with skeptics. If r/Supplements earn-in stalls, Stage 1–2 of Lane B has no channel — and Lane A loses its most powerful trust marker. This is the single biggest execution risk in the map.
  • Retention content is the least-built part of the plan — the streak/bottle-cycle mechanic is named everywhere but specified nowhere. It's the load-bearing wall of the only stage that matters. Spec it before launch, not after the first refills lapse.
